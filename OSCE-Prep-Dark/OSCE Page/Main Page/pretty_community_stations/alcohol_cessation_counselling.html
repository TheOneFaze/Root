High-Yield OSCE Points üß†

üí¨ Assess & Advise

  • Begin with a non-judgemental opening.
  • Use a validated tool like the AUDIT questionnaire to assess consumption and risk level.
  • Clearly explain the results and what the risk category (e.g., hazardous, harmful, possible dependence) means.
  • Advise on the long-term health risks associated with their level of drinking.

ü§î Explore Motivation (ICE)

  • Ideas: Ask what the patient thinks about their drinking and its impact.
  • Concerns: Explore their main worries about their drinking or about the prospect of cutting down/stopping.
  • Expectations: Ask what they hope to achieve (e.g., moderation vs. abstinence).
  • Assess readiness to change.

üìã Develop a Plan (Assist)

  • For Moderation: Suggest practical strategies like setting weekly unit goals (e.g., under 14 units), having drink-free days, switching to lower-strength drinks, and keeping a drink diary.
  • For Abstinence (Quitting): This is the goal for anyone with dependence.
    • CRUCIAL: Assess for risk of severe withdrawal (e.g., history of seizures, delirium tremens).
    • Explain that for dependent drinkers, stopping abruptly without medical support can be dangerous.

ü§ù Discuss Support Options

  • Psychosocial (Essential):
    • Strongly recommend and refer to local specialist drug and alcohol services.
    • Mention mutual aid groups as an option (e.g., Alcoholics Anonymous, SMART Recovery).
  • Pharmacological (Post-Detox):
    • Mention medications that can help prevent relapse after a successful withdrawal.
    • Options include Acamprosate (reduces cravings) or Naltrexone (reduces the reward from alcohol).
    • Disulfiram (causes a severe reaction to alcohol) is a specialist option.

üö® Arrange Follow-up & Safety Net

  • Arrange a follow-up appointment to review progress.
  • Safety Net (for those quitting from dependence): Clearly explain symptoms of severe withdrawal (e.g., profuse sweating, severe shaking, confusion, hallucinations, seizures).
  • Instruct the patient to seek immediate medical help (call 999 or go to A&E) if severe withdrawal symptoms occur.

üí¨ Opening the Conversation

"Thank you for coming in today. I understand you wanted to talk about your alcohol consumption. It takes courage to have this conversation, and it's a really positive first step."

"To help me give you the best advice, would it be okay if I asked you a few standard questions about your drinking habits? It's a confidential questionnaire called the AUDIT, and it helps us understand your level of risk."

"What are your thoughts on that? What's prompted you to think about making a change at this time?"

"Once you have stopped drinking, there are also effective medications we can prescribe. These aren't a cure, but they can support your recovery. For example, some medications work by reducing your cravings for alcohol, which makes it easier to stay abstinent."

"Let's make a plan together now. We can complete the referral to the support service and book a follow-up appointment here for one to two weeks' time to check in and see how you are."
